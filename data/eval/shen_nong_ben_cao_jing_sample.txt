SHEN NONG BEN CAO JING — Selected Entries (English Translation)

Entry 1: Ma Huang (Ephedra sinica)
Ma Huang, also known as Ephedra or "hemp yellow herb" (麻黄), is bitter in taste and warm in nature. It governs stroke-induced coughs and ascending qi. It disperses cold pathogenic factors and opens the pores. It is used to treat intermittent fever, sweating disorders, and nasal congestion. When prepared as a decoction boiled three times, it resolves chills and fever. It grows in mountainous regions and is harvested in autumn.

Entry 2: Huang Qi (Astragalus membranaceus)
Huang Qi, known as Astragalus or "yellow leader" (黄芪), is sweet in taste and slightly warm in nature. It supplements deficiency and strengthens the exterior. It is the chief herb for treating spontaneous sweating, fatigue, and chronic weakness. It is effective against edema and promotes urination. It expels pus from sores and generates flesh for wound healing. Prepared as a decoction or dried powder taken with warm water. It nourishes the spleen and lung qi.

Entry 3: Gan Cao (Glycyrrhiza uralensis)
Gan Cao, also called Licorice Root or "sweet herb" (甘草), is sweet in taste and neutral in nature. It is the great harmonizer — used in the majority of prescriptions to moderate and harmonize the properties of other herbs. On its own, it treats sore throat, cough, and abdominal pain. It is an antidote for various poisons. Honey-processed licorice (炙甘草) strengthens the spleen and supplements qi. Raw licorice clears heat and relieves toxicity. It is prepared as decoction, powder, or chewed directly.

Entry 4: Qing Hao (Artemisia annua)
Qing Hao, known as Sweet Wormwood or Annual Wormwood (青蒿), is bitter in taste and cold in nature. It treats intermittent fevers and alternating chills and heat — the condition the ancients called "nue" (疟, malaria-like illness). Ge Hong's Zhou Hou Bei Ji Fang records: "Take a handful of qinghao, soak in two sheng of water, wring out the juice, and drink it all" (青蒿一握，以水二升渍，绞取汁，尽服之). It clears deficiency heat and summer heat. It cools the blood and stops bleeding. It is not to be boiled — the cold-water extraction preserves its active properties.

Entry 5: Ren Shen (Panax ginseng)
Ren Shen, known as Ginseng or "man root" (人参), is sweet and slightly bitter in taste, warm in nature. It is the supreme herb for supplementing the original qi. It treats severe exhaustion, shortness of breath, and failing pulse. It calms the spirit and benefits wisdom. It generates fluids and quenches thirst. It is used for chronic illness with deficiency, palpitations, insomnia, and poor appetite. Prepared as decoction, sliced and steeped, or steamed with honey. It is contraindicated with Veratrum (Li Lu / 藜芦).

Entry 6: Da Huang (Rheum palmatum)
Da Huang, known as Rhubarb or "great yellow" (大黄), is bitter in taste and cold in nature. It is the general herb for purging — it powerfully drains heat accumulation, moves stagnant blood, and unblocks the bowels. It treats constipation with abdominal fullness, jaundice with dark urine, and blood stasis causing amenorrhea. Externally applied as a poultice, it reduces swelling from burns and toxic sores. Prepared as decoction (added late to preserve purgative strength) or ground into powder. Wine-processed rhubarb (酒大黄) enhances blood-moving properties.
